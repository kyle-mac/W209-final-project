Exploring Cryptocurrency Expansion

Introduction:

This page presents a guided tour of the Cryptocurrency Ecosystem, and particularly BitCoin. Starting with the history and the rise of Public Interest, we examine Global Interest in BitCoin expressed in terms of legality and volume of Trade occurring in each country's currency over time. The subsequent section wrestles with whether BitCoin is perceived and traded more as a currency or as an asset. We compare how the BitCoin fares as an asset compared to other financial indicators including Gold, the VIX (Market Fear Index) and the Stock Market. Finally, the last section presents how the Price of BitCoin and volume traded by country respond to events such as government regulations.

A Brief History

In 2008 an unknown author releases a paper (Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System) under the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto that highlights the potential of a cryptocurrency called Bitcoin to be revolutionize modern payment systems in . A year later Bitcoin mining begins and the landscape of cryptocurrencies begins to expand.

Rise and Fall

The cryptocurrency market has seen continued expansion since Bitcoin was first introduced in 2009. While early years showed slow growth and expansion, the popularity of Bitcoin and other currencies exploded in 2017 before crashing shortly after 2018.

Asset or Currency?

If we expect per the initial idea presented in Satoshi Nakamoto’s paper that BitCoin will become a decentralized world currency, that countries with stable currencies would have a slow uptake of compared to those with weak, volatile currencies. The chart below presents the correlation between Exchange Rates and Volume of BitCoin traded for a given country over time.

The pattern suggest that as the exchange rate increases (the currency gets weaker), the volume of BitCoin traded in that currency is higher. However, this may also be mainly coming from countries with weaker currencies overall that might be using it as a store of value.

Another indicator that Bitcoin is emerging more as an asset than a currency comes from looking at how countries classify BitCoin: as a commodity, property, barter good or currency. The following graph shows that most of the BitCoin Volume Traded comes from countries which classify it as a commodity (China )or Property (US). This also shows true in the time periods featuring the highest positive outliers in trade. Use the time filter to explore the classification under which the most volume of BitCoin is being traded. Use the box plot to see the distribution of BitCoin prices in the corresponding period.

If we believe then that BitCoin is more an asset than an emerging world currency, it makes sense to compare how it fares alongside major financial indicators.The following line chart shows price volatility as a daily percent change in Price for financial indicators such as Gold, Stock Market, US Dollar and the VIX (Market Fear Index).

As an asset, we see that BitCoin has no apparent correlation with gold prices and stock market prices. Both indicators show a cyclical change in price. For Bitcoin however, there are periods of low volatility and high volatility with no apparent pattern. Again the peaks and troughs are much higher than the other goods. While we might explain this by arguing that BitCoin is a new “asset” which hasn’t stabilized yet, there is also no correlation with the Market Fear Index or VIX. This shows that even as a new asset, the pattern of investment and the subsequently rising price is not following the pattern of confidence in the market. It then seems more of a speculative good which depends on public interest rather than the market strength.
